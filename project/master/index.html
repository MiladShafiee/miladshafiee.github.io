Master Thesis

 

Push recovery of a humanoid robot under severe external disturbances

2015 â€“ 2017

I passed my master thesis under the supervision of Prof. Aghil Yousefi-Koma and my goal was to employ whole capability of a biped robot for balance recovery. In this regard my work can be devided into two parts:

  • I developed a human-inspired feedback controller for push recovery of Mini-SURENA humanoid robot

  • We deploy model predictive control and concept of Divergent Component of motion to developing a Push recovery Controller during walking and also in stance mode.

Part 1: Human-inspired feedback controller for balance recovery of SURENA Mini humanoid robot

In this part of research, a combination of ankle and hip strategy is used for push recovery of a position-controlled humanoid robot. Ankle strategy and hip strategy are equivalent to Center of Pressure (CoP) and Centroidal Moment Pivot (CMP) regulation respectively. For controlling the CMP and CoP we need a torque-controlled robot, however most of the conventional humanoid robots are position controlled. In this regard, we present an efficient way for implementation of the hip and ankle strategies on a position controlled humanoid robot. We employ a feedback controller to compensate the capture point error. Using our scheme, a simple and practical push recovery controller is designed which can be implemented on the most of the conventional humanoid robots without the need for torque sensors. Part 2: Robust Bipedal Locomotion Control Based on Model Predictive Control and Divergent Component of Motion

In this part, we employ a single MPC which uses a combination of Center of Pressure (CoP) manipulation, step adjustment, and Centroidal Moment Pivot (CMP) modulation to design a robust walking controller. The main contribution of our work is inclusion of angular momentum in DCM dynamic. Using our scheme, a general and robust walking controller is designed which can be implemented on robots with different control authorities, for walking on various environments, e.g. uneven terrains or surfaces with a very limited feasible area for stepping such as rocks. Following figure shows the result of balance recovery for walking on limited contact surfaces. The inner gray rectangular shows the contact surface and the light pink rectangular shows the foot of robot. In this scenario, a push with the magnitude of 250 N during 0.05 s in sagittal direction is exerted on the CoM of the robot at start of walking. The MPC saves the robot from falling by genration of angular momentum.

In the standing mode push recovery scenario, a severe push is exerted on the CoM of the robot. As we expected, the large push throws the CP out of the support polygon, and the ZMP(CoP) cannot navigate it. Therefore, the angular momentum is generated by the MPC to move the CMP outside the support polygon for controlling the CP.
In the second scenario, the robot stands on one leg, while the contact surface is shrunken to a line or a point. Two examples for this situation are standing on lumber and standing on rock. In this case, the CMP modulation recovers the robot from the disturbance, because the support polygon is too small and the ankle strategy is not helpful anymore.
